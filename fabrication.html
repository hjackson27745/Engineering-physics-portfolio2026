Project Overview

This project was completed as part of a summer fabrication course and involved designing and building a physical system from the ground up. More than just a final class assignment, it was a hands-on experience in translating an idea into a real object while working through the practical realities of fabrication.

What makes this project especially meaningful to me is that it required both technical thinking and direct engagement with the build process. It pushed me to refine my design, troubleshoot unexpected challenges, and think carefully about how choices in materials, construction, and geometry affect final performance.

Goal

The goal of this project was to design and fabricate a functional smart plant pot that helps users monitor plant health through real-time environmental feedback. One of the main components is a soil moisture sensor that detects three moisture conditions: dry, moist, and wet. Each level is represented by a corresponding LED indicator light—red signals soil that is too dry, green indicates an ideal moisture level, and blue shows that the soil is overly wet. An additional humidity sensor was also incorporated into the design, with a yellow LED added after the initial prototype stage to indicate surrounding humidity conditions. Another key feature of the project is the OLED display screen, which provides a clear and condensed overview of the sensor readings. This interactive display allows users to better understand their plant’s needs and make informed decisions about watering and overall plant care based on moisture and humidity data.

This meant thinking about the project at multiple levels: the conceptual idea, the physical design, the fabrication process itself, and the adjustments needed once the real build introduced practical constraints.

Design and Fabrication Process

The project began with an initial concept and was developed through a series of design choices related to structure, materials, and fabrication strategy. As I moved from planning into construction, I had to think carefully about how the build would actually come together in practice rather than only in theory.

One of the most valuable parts of the process was learning how to connect design intent with real implementation. Fabrication required attention to detail, patience, and flexibility, especially when small decisions affected the final assembly and performance of the system.

Challenges and Iteration

A major reason this is one of my favorite projects is that it involved genuine iteration. As with many hands-on projects, not everything worked perfectly on the first attempt. The fabrication process revealed issues that were not obvious at the design stage, which meant I had to troubleshoot, revise, and adapt.

Working through those challenges was one of the most rewarding parts of the project. It taught me how important it is to treat setbacks as part of the engineering process rather than as failures. Revising the design and improving the build deepened my understanding of how physical systems behave in practice.

Why I Am Proud of This Project

This project stands out to me because it best reflects the type of work I most enjoy: hands-on, iterative, and grounded in building something tangible. It combined design, fabrication, and problem-solving in a way that felt both challenging and deeply engaging.

I am especially proud of this project because it represents growth in the way I approach technical work. It strengthened my confidence in building and troubleshooting physical systems, and it showed me how much I enjoy work that brings together theory, experimentation, and real-world implementation.
